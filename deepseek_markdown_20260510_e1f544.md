| Beyond the Final Artifact: A Quantitative Analysis of Human- LLM Interaction Throughout the Attribute- Driven Design (ADD) Process | Além do Artefato Final: Uma Análise Quantitativa da Interação Humano‑LLM ao Longo do Processo de Projeto Orientado a Atributos (ADD) |
| Paulo Sergio da Soledade Cerqueira | Paulo Sergio da Soledade Cerqueira |
| Federal University of Bahia, Brazil | Universidade Federal da Bahia, Brasil |
| Abstract | Resumo |
| Background. Large language models (LLMs) are rarely evaluated within software architecture design– a discipline requiring complex, abstract reasoning. Current literature primarily compares final outputs, overlooking the iterative co- creation process where architects refine AI- generated suggestions. Aims. This study investigates how architects modify LLM- generated artifacts across the nine structured steps of Attribute- Driven Design (ADD), identifies emergent modification patterns, and examines their relationship with final artifact quality. Method. In an observational study, 30 pairs designed a distributed storage system. At each ADD step, pairs received and refined LLM suggestions. We quantified intervention magnitude, strategic diversity, and structural orientation using contextual embeddings and textual overlap metrics. Results. During initial requirements phases, pairs predominantly accepted LLM outputs with minimal intervention, aligning with the model’s proficiency in descriptive tasks. Transitioning to architectural design triggered a behavioral divide: participants executed intensive structural reorganizations and discarded more generated content. High‑ performing pairs intervened selectively when artifacts degraded, whereas low‑ performing teams remained passive. Notably, no pair fully replaced an LLM‑ generated structure, demonstrating a systemic anchoring to the AI’s original framework. Conclusions. Human‑ LLM collaboration in architecture design is not uniform: it depends on task complexity, process stage, and team engagement. Quality depends not on the volume of changes but on timely, selective intervention. Our findings provide a taxonomy of modification patterns and offer grounding for AI‑ assisted architecture education and tool design. | Contexto. Modelos de Linguagem de Grande Escala (LLMs) raramente são avaliados no contexto de projeto de arquitetura de software – uma disciplina que exige raciocínio complexo e abstrato. A literatura atual compara primordialmente os artefatos finais, ignorando o processo iterativo de cocriação em que arquitetos refinam sugestões geradas por IA. Objetivos. Este estudo investiga como arquitetos modificam artefatos gerados por LLM ao longo das nove etapas estruturadas do Projeto Orientado a Atributos (ADD), identifica padrões emergentes de modificação e examina sua relação com a qualidade final do artefato. Método. Em um estudo observacional, 30 duplas projetaram um sistema de armazenamento distribuído. Em cada etapa do ADD, as duplas receberam e refinaram sugestões do LLM. Quantificamos a magnitude da intervenção, a diversidade estratégica e a orientação estrutural utilizando embeddings contextuais e métricas de sobreposição textual. Resultados. Durante as fases iniciais de requisitos, as duplas aceitaram predominantemente as saídas do LLM com mínima intervenção, alinhando‑se à proficiência do modelo em tarefas descritivas. A transição para o projeto arquitetural desencadeou uma divisão comportamental: os participantes executaram reorganizações estruturais intensivas e descartaram mais conteúdo gerado. Duplas de alto desempenho intervieram seletivamente quando os artefatos se degradavam, enquanto equipes de baixo desempenho permaneceram passivas. Notavelmente, nenhuma dupla substituiu totalmente uma estrutura gerada pelo LLM, demonstrando uma ancoragem sistêmica ao arcabouço original da IA. Conclusões. A colaboração humano‑LLM no projeto de arquitetura não é uniforme: depende da complexidade da tarefa, da etapa do processo e do engajamento da equipe. A qualidade não depende do volume de alterações, mas da intervenção oportuna e seletiva. Nossos achados fornecem uma taxonomia dos padrões de modificação e oferecem fundamentos para a educação em arquitetura assistida por IA e para o projeto de ferramentas. |

| 76 Related Work 77 The overwhelming majority of research on LLMs in software engineering focuses on the imple‑ 78 mentation and quality assurance phases, such as code generation [20, 27, 26], summarization 79 [23], defect detection, and testing [2]. Recent systematic reviews indicate that studies focused 80 on the design and architecture phase represent a tiny fraction (less than \(1\%\) ) of primary 81 output [11]. Works such as those by Guerra & Ernst [10] and Soliman et al. [19] represent 82 exceptions, investigating front‑ end architectural knowledge and comparing multiple LLMs, 83 respectively. Guerra & Ernst demonstrated that while LLMs excel in higher‑ order cognitive 84 tasks (e.g., evaluating and creating structures), they struggle with lower‑ order tasks like 85 retrieving precise architectural details. Similarly, Soliman et al. found that LLMs generally 86 exhibit high recall but low precision in architectural queries, generating many false positives 87 that require careful expert filtering. However, software architecture remains an area lacking 88 observational data, especially data that captures the iterative design process. 89 Studies that begin to explore the use of AI in software architecture tend to compare 90 AI‑ generated decisions with human decisions at a single point of evaluation. For example, 91 Dhar et al. [8] investigated whether LLMs can generate architectural design decisions, finding 92 that models like GPT‑ 4 can produce relevant zero‑ shot decisions but still fall significantly 93 short of human‑ level performance. Tagliaferro et al. [21] evaluated the automation of design 94 from informal specifications. Other works focus on architecture retrieval from source code 95 [12, 1] or on the detection of architectural smells [22], where Tessa et al. observed that 96 although LLMs can achieve perfect recall in detecting Hub‑ like dependencies, their precision 97 drops significantly on complex smells, and their generated explanations often lack depth 98 and context. Although important, these studies treat LLM as a static generator, not as an 99 assistant in a collaborative and iterative workflow. To date, we have not found studies that 100 map how humans (students or professionals) modify AI suggestions over time – whether they 101 simply accept, correct, expand, or reorganize the content – to build the final artifact. 102 The Attribute‑ Driven Design (ADD) method is a well‑ established process for system‑ 103 atically designing software architectures, guided by architectural drivers (non‑ functional 104 requirements and constraints). | Clarification on the “structural preservation bias” construct. It is important to clarify that the term structural preservation bias is proposed here as an emergent, domain‑ specific label for a pattern observed in our study, rather than as a well‑ established construct in the software architecture literature. The observed behavior aligns with two well‑ documented cognitive phenomena. First, it resonates with automation bias, where users over‑ rely on automated suggestions, a tendency that can be exacerbated by cognitive load and the design of AI feedback mechanisms [4]. Second, it reflects anchoring bias, as the LLM’s initial output can serve as an anchor that constrains the exploration of alternative designs; recent work has shown that LLM‑ generated suggestions can anchor human judgment and reduce analytical depth [7]. While these biases have been extensively studied in decision‑ making and human‑ computer interaction, their specific manifestation in iterative LLM‑ assisted architectural design has not been previously characterized. Therefore, this proposal should be viewed as a first, empirically grounded step toward consolidating this construct. | Esclarecimento sobre o construto “viés de preservação estrutural”. O comportamento observado alinha‑se com dois fenômenos cognitivos bem documentados.
